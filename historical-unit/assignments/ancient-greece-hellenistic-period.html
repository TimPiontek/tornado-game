Ancient Greece - Hellenistic Period

Historical Context

Setting: Mediterranean and Near East, 323-31 BCE. The period following Alexander the Great's death, marked by the spread of Greek culture and the rise of Hellenistic kingdoms.
What Happens: The Hellenistic Period begins with the death of Alexander the Great in 323 BCE and ends with the Roman conquest of Egypt in 31 BCE. Alexander's generals divide his empire into Hellenistic kingdoms including the Seleucid Empire, Ptolemaic Egypt, and the Antigonid Kingdom of Macedonia. Greek culture spreads throughout the known world, creating a cosmopolitan civilization that blends Greek, Persian, Egyptian, and other cultures. This period sees advances in science, philosophy, art, and literature. The Hellenistic world eventually falls to the expanding Roman Empire.
Key Figures: Alexander the Great (Macedonian king), Ptolemy I (founder of Ptolemaic Egypt), Seleucus I (founder of Seleucid Empire), Antigonus I (founder of Antigonid dynasty), Cleopatra VII (last Ptolemaic ruler), Archimedes (mathematician and inventor), Euclid (mathematician), Eratosthenes (geographer), Aristarchus (astronomer), Hipparchus (astronomer), Epicurus (philosopher), Zeno (philosopher), Menander (playwright), Theocritus (poet).
