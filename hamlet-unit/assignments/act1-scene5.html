Hamlet Act 1, Scene 5: The Ghost's Revelation

Scene Summary

The ghost reveals to Hamlet that he was murdered by Claudius, who poured poison in his ear while he slept. The ghost demands that Hamlet avenge his murder but warns him not to harm his mother, Gertrude. Hamlet swears to avenge his father's death and makes his companions swear to secrecy about what they have seen. This revelation sets the central conflict of the play in motion.

Key Characters in This Scene

  • Hamlet - The prince, learning the truth about his father's death
  • The Ghost - The spirit of King Hamlet, revealing the murder
  • Horatio - Hamlet's friend, witnessing the revelation
  • Marcellus - A guard, also witnessing the scene

Multiple Choice Questions

1. The Ghost tells Hamlet, "I am thy father's spirit, doomed for a certain term to walk the night." What is the significance of this revelation?
Answer: C. It establishes a connection between sin and punishment after death.
Standard: RL.9-10.2 – Determine theme and analyze its development.
2. When the Ghost commands Hamlet to "revenge his foul and most unnatural murder," what is the ghost implying?
Answer: B. Claudius killed him in an act against nature and morality.
Standard: RL.9-10.3 – Character motivations.
3. The Ghost says of Gertrude, "Leave her to heaven." What does this suggest about the Ghost's view of Gertrude?
Answer: C. He suggests she is morally weak but not criminally guilty.
Standard: RL.9-10.4 – Analyze connotative meaning.

Short Answer Questions

4. Short Answer: How does Hamlet's vow "Adieu, adieu! remember me" mark a turning point in his character development?
Answer: Hamlet commits himself fully to avenging his father, marking his transformation from passive mourner to active agent. This vow initiates the central conflict: his moral hesitation versus his duty.
Standard: RL.9-10.3 – Analyze character change.
5. Short Answer: Explain the significance of Hamlet saying he will "wipe away all trivial fond records… and thy commandment all alone shall live." What does this figurative imagery show?
Answer: This metaphor compares Hamlet's mind to a recording tablet, suggesting he will erase all distractions to focus solely on revenge. It reveals obsession and foreshadows internal turmoil.
Standard: RL.9-10.4 – Interpret figurative language.
