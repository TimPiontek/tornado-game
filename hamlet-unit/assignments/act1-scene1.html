Hamlet Act 1, Scene 1: The Ghost Appears

Scene Summary

The play opens on the battlements of Elsinore Castle in Denmark. It is midnight, and Francisco, a guard, is being relieved by Bernardo. The two guards discuss the strange events of the past few nights, during which they have seen what appears to be the ghost of the recently deceased King Hamlet. Marcellus and Horatio arrive, and Horatio, a scholar and Hamlet's close friend, is skeptical about the ghost's existence. However, when the ghost appears, Horatio recognizes it as the late king and attempts to speak to it. The ghost does not respond and disappears at dawn. This supernatural encounter sets the tone for the play and introduces the central mystery of King Hamlet's death.

Key Characters in This Scene

  • Francisco - A guard on duty
  • Bernardo - A guard who has seen the ghost
  • Marcellus - Another guard who has witnessed the ghost
  • Horatio - Hamlet's friend and a scholar, skeptical of the supernatural
  • The Ghost - The spirit of King Hamlet, Hamlet's father

Multiple Choice Questions

1. What time of day does the ghost first appear in Act 1, Scene 1?
Answer: Midnight
2. Who is the first character to see the ghost of King Hamlet?
Answer: Bernardo
3. What is Horatio's initial reaction to the guards' claims about seeing a ghost?
Answer: He is skeptical and doesn't believe them
4. What happens when Horatio tries to speak to the ghost?
Answer: The ghost does not respond and disappears
5. What literary device is primarily used to establish the mood in this opening scene?
Answer: Foreshadowing

Short Answer Questions

6. Explain how Shakespeare uses the supernatural element in this opening scene to establish the play's central conflict.
Answer: The ghost's appearance suggests that King Hamlet's death was unnatural and that there is unfinished business. This supernatural element introduces the central conflict of the play - the mystery surrounding King Hamlet's death and the need for justice or revenge.
7. Analyze the significance of Horatio's character in this scene. Why is it important that he, specifically, witnesses the ghost?
Answer: Horatio is a scholar and Hamlet's close friend, making him a credible witness. His skepticism initially, followed by his confirmation of the ghost's existence, lends credibility to the supernatural events. As an educated man, his testimony carries more weight than that of the guards alone.
8. What does the ghost's silence when Horatio speaks to it suggest about the nature of the spirit and its purpose?
Answer: The ghost's silence suggests it has a specific purpose and audience - it may only be meant to communicate with Hamlet, not with others. This creates anticipation for Hamlet's eventual encounter with the ghost and suggests the spirit has unfinished business that only Hamlet can address.
9. How does Shakespeare use the setting (time and place) to enhance the supernatural atmosphere of this scene?
Answer: The midnight setting on the castle battlements creates an eerie, mysterious atmosphere. The darkness and isolation of the location enhance the supernatural elements, while the castle setting suggests political intrigue and hidden secrets that will be revealed throughout the play.
10. Identify and explain one example of foreshadowing in this scene that hints at future events in the play.
Answer: The ghost's appearance and its resemblance to King Hamlet foreshadows the revelation that King Hamlet was murdered. This supernatural visitation suggests that there is unfinished business related to his death, which will drive the plot forward as Hamlet seeks to uncover the truth and avenge his father.

Analysis Questions

11. Analyze the dramatic function of this opening scene. How does it serve as an effective introduction to the play?
Answer: This scene serves multiple dramatic functions: it establishes the supernatural element that will drive the plot, introduces the central mystery of King Hamlet's death, creates anticipation for Hamlet's character, and sets the dark, mysterious tone that pervades the play. It also provides exposition about recent events while creating suspense about what will happen next.
12. Compare and contrast the reactions of the guards versus Horatio to the ghost's appearance. What does this reveal about their characters and the play's themes?
Answer: The guards are immediately frightened and believe what they see, while Horatio is initially skeptical but becomes convinced through direct observation. This contrast highlights themes of knowledge versus belief, the role of education and reason, and the tension between the supernatural and the rational world that will be central to Hamlet's character development.
13. Examine the language and imagery used in this scene. How does Shakespeare's word choice contribute to the atmosphere and mood?
Answer: Shakespeare uses dark, ominous imagery ("dead hour," "dreadful summit") and repetitive, anxious dialogue to create tension. The guards' fragmented, nervous speech contrasts with Horatio's more measured, scholarly language, emphasizing the supernatural nature of their experience and the intellectual approach needed to understand it.
14. How does this scene establish the play's central themes of appearance versus reality and the nature of truth?
Answer: The ghost's appearance blurs the line between the natural and supernatural worlds, raising questions about what is real and what is illusion. The guards' conflicting accounts and Horatio's changing perspective demonstrate the difficulty of determining truth, a theme that will be central to Hamlet's struggle throughout the play.
15. Evaluate the effectiveness of Shakespeare's choice to begin the play with this supernatural encounter rather than introducing Hamlet directly.
Answer: Beginning with the ghost creates immediate intrigue and establishes the supernatural framework before introducing the protagonist. This approach allows the audience to understand the context and mystery that will drive Hamlet's actions, making his eventual encounter with the ghost more meaningful and his subsequent behavior more understandable.
