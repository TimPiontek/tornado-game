Hamlet Act 3, Scene 1: The Nunnery Scene

Scene Summary

Claudius and Polonius hide to spy on Hamlet and Ophelia. Hamlet enters, delivering his famous 'To be or not to be' soliloquy, contemplating suicide and the nature of existence. When Ophelia approaches, Hamlet becomes hostile and cruel, telling her to go to a nunnery and denying that he ever loved her. This scene reveals Hamlet's conflicted feelings and his plan to use madness as a cover for his investigation.

Key Characters in This Scene

  • Claudius - The king, hiding to spy on Hamlet
  • Polonius - The counselor, also spying on Hamlet
  • Ophelia - Polonius's daughter, confronting Hamlet
  • Hamlet - The prince, delivering his famous soliloquy

Multiple Choice Questions

1. In the soliloquy, Hamlet says, "To die, to sleep— / To sleep, perchance to dream." What is the main idea Hamlet is contemplating?
Answer: B. Whether death may bring peace or unknown suffering
Standard: RL.9-10.2 – Analyze central idea.
2. When Ophelia returns Hamlet's "remembrances," Hamlet replies, "I did love you once." What does this reveal about Hamlet's emotional state?
Answer: C. He is conflicted and pushing Ophelia away as part of his plan
Standard: RL.9-10.3 – Character development.
3. Claudius says, "Madness in great ones must not unwatched go." What tone does this line communicate?
Answer: B. Fearful suspicion
Standard: RL.9-10.4 – Tone analysis.

Short Answer Questions

4. Short Answer: How does Hamlet use figurative language in the line "slings and arrows of outrageous fortune" to express his inner conflict?
Answer: Hamlet uses a metaphor comparing life's hardships to weapons attacking him. This imagery reflects his feeling that suffering is an external force he must endure or escape, showing his despair.
Standard: RL.9-10.4 – Figurative language.
5. Short Answer: What does Ophelia's lament, "O, what a noble mind is here o'erthrown!" reveal about her perception of Hamlet?
Answer: Ophelia sees Hamlet as a tragic figure whose brilliance has been destroyed by grief. Her words illustrate the theme of appearance vs. reality as she mourns the loss of who Hamlet once was.
Standard: RL.9-10.3 – Character analysis.
