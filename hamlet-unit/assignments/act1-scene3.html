Hamlet Act 1, Scene 3: Laertes' Departure

Scene Summary

Laertes prepares to return to France and gives advice to his sister Ophelia about her relationship with Hamlet. He warns her that Hamlet's love for her may not be genuine, as princes often cannot choose their own spouses. Polonius enters and gives Laertes lengthy advice about how to behave in France, including the famous 'to thine own self be true' speech. After Laertes leaves, Polonius questions Ophelia about her relationship with Hamlet and forbids her from seeing him, claiming that Hamlet's advances are not serious.

Key Characters in This Scene

  • Laertes - Polonius's son, preparing to return to France
  • Ophelia - Polonius's daughter, in love with Hamlet
  • Polonius - The chief counselor, father of Laertes and Ophelia

Multiple Choice Questions

1. Laertes warns Ophelia that Hamlet's "will is not his own." What does he mean?
Answer: B. Hamlet must marry for political reasons, not personal desire.
Standard: RL.9-10.3 – Analyze character motivations.
2. Polonius tells Ophelia to "tender yourself more dearly." What figurative technique is Shakespeare using?
Answer: B. Metaphor comparing Ophelia to an object of value
Standard: RL.9-10.4 – Analyze language and its impact on meaning.
3. When Polonius says to Laertes, "To thine own self be true," what theme is being emphasized?
Answer: B. Personal integrity is central to navigating the world successfully.
Standard: RL.9-10.2 – Theme development.

Short Answer Questions

4. Short Answer: How does Laertes's advice to Ophelia reflect gender expectations during the Elizabethan era?
Answer: Laertes tells Ophelia to protect her chastity because her value is tied to her purity, reflecting societal norms where women's reputations affected family honor: "Then weigh what loss your honor may sustain."
Standard: RL.9-10.9 – Analyze how a text reflects historical context.
5. Short Answer: In what way does Polonius's treatment of Ophelia reveal his character?
Answer: Polonius is controlling and sees Ophelia as a tool for political advantage. By saying she has "ta'en these tenders for true pay," he mistrusts her judgment and prioritizes reputation over her emotions.
Standard: RL.9-10.3 – Characterization.
