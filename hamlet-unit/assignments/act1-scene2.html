Hamlet Act 1, Scene 2: The Court Scene

Scene Summary

This scene takes place in the royal court of Denmark. King Claudius addresses the court, acknowledging both the death of his brother King Hamlet and his recent marriage to Queen Gertrude. He also deals with the threat from young Fortinbras of Norway. Hamlet is introduced for the first time, appearing in mourning clothes and expressing his deep grief over his father's death and his mother's hasty remarriage. Claudius and Gertrude try to convince Hamlet to stop mourning and accept the new situation. Hamlet's first soliloquy reveals his suicidal thoughts and his disgust with the world, particularly his mother's quick remarriage to his uncle.

Key Characters in This Scene

  • King Claudius - The new king, Hamlet's uncle and stepfather
  • Queen Gertrude - Hamlet's mother, recently married to Claudius
  • Hamlet - The prince, mourning his father's death
  • Polonius - The chief counselor to the king
  • Laertes - Polonius's son, asking permission to return to France
  • Voltemand & Cornelius - Courtiers sent to Norway

Multiple Choice Questions

1. What does Claudius announce at the beginning of this scene?
Answer: His marriage to Gertrude and his ascension to the throne
2. How does Hamlet appear when he first enters the scene?
Answer: In mourning clothes, visibly grieving
3. What does Claudius tell Hamlet about mourning his father?
Answer: That it is unmanly and he should stop grieving
4. What does Hamlet reveal in his first soliloquy?
Answer: His suicidal thoughts and disgust with the world
5. What literary device does Shakespeare use in Hamlet's soliloquy "O, that this too too solid flesh would melt"?
Answer: Metaphor

Short Answer Questions

6. Analyze the contrast between Claudius's public speech and Hamlet's private soliloquy. What does this reveal about their characters?
Answer: Claudius's speech is formal, political, and focused on moving forward, while Hamlet's soliloquy is emotional, personal, and focused on the past. This contrast reveals Claudius as pragmatic and politically minded, while Hamlet is introspective and emotionally overwhelmed by grief.
7. Explain the significance of Hamlet's mourning clothes in this scene. What does this symbolize?
Answer: Hamlet's mourning clothes symbolize his continued grief and refusal to accept the new political reality. They represent his emotional state and his unwillingness to move on from his father's death, contrasting with the court's expectation that he should accept Claudius as king.
8. How does Shakespeare establish the theme of appearance versus reality in this scene?
Answer: The scene contrasts Claudius's public facade of normalcy and political stability with Hamlet's private emotional turmoil. Claudius presents a united front while Hamlet reveals the underlying tension and grief that threatens the court's stability.
9. What does Hamlet's relationship with his mother reveal about his character and the play's themes?
Answer: Hamlet's relationship with his mother reveals his deep emotional attachment to family and his struggle with betrayal. His disgust at her quick remarriage introduces themes of loyalty, betrayal, and the corruption of familial bonds that will drive the plot.
10. Analyze the political subplot involving Fortinbras. How does this relate to the main plot?
Answer: The Fortinbras subplot provides external political pressure that mirrors Hamlet's internal conflict. Both young men have lost fathers and seek to restore honor, creating parallel storylines that will intersect later in the play and highlight themes of revenge and political instability.

Analysis Questions

11. Examine Hamlet's first soliloquy as a window into his psyche. What does it reveal about his mental state and worldview?
Answer: Hamlet's soliloquy reveals a deeply depressed and disillusioned young man who sees the world as corrupt and meaningless. His suicidal thoughts and disgust with his mother's actions show a character overwhelmed by grief and moral outrage, setting up his later struggles with action and inaction.
12. Compare Claudius's approach to grief and mourning with Hamlet's. What does this contrast suggest about their different philosophies?
Answer: Claudius advocates for moving forward and accepting change, while Hamlet clings to the past and refuses to accept the new reality. This contrast suggests different approaches to life: Claudius represents pragmatism and political expediency, while Hamlet represents idealism and emotional authenticity.
13. How does Shakespeare use dramatic irony in this scene to create tension and foreshadow future events?
Answer: The audience knows from the previous scene that King Hamlet's ghost has appeared, suggesting foul play, while the court remains unaware. This creates dramatic irony as Claudius presents himself as a legitimate king while the audience suspects otherwise, foreshadowing Hamlet's eventual discovery of the truth.
14. Analyze the language and imagery in Hamlet's soliloquy. How does Shakespeare's word choice contribute to the emotional impact?
Answer: Shakespeare uses vivid, visceral imagery ("solid flesh would melt," "unweeded garden") and repetitive, anguished language to convey Hamlet's emotional turmoil. The contrast between his elevated, poetic language and his base emotions creates a powerful effect that draws the audience into his psychological state.
15. Evaluate how this scene functions as character development for Hamlet. What aspects of his personality are established here?
Answer: This scene establishes Hamlet as an introspective, emotionally complex character who values authenticity over political expediency. His deep grief, moral sensitivity, and tendency toward philosophical reflection are all established here, creating the foundation for his later struggles with action, revenge, and moral responsibility.
