Hamlet Act 3, Scene 3: Claudius at Prayer

Scene Summary

Claudius, alone and guilt-ridden, attempts to pray for forgiveness but realizes he cannot truly repent while still enjoying the benefits of his crime. Hamlet enters and sees Claudius praying, but decides not to kill him because he believes killing Claudius while he is praying would send him to heaven. This scene reveals Hamlet's moral complexity and his tendency to overthink his actions.

Key Characters in This Scene

  • Claudius - The king, struggling with guilt and prayer
  • Hamlet - The prince, hesitating to kill Claudius

Multiple Choice Questions

1. Why doesn't Hamlet kill Claudius while he is praying?
Answer: B. Hamlet wants him to suffer eternally, not die in a state of grace.
Standard: RL.9-10.3 – Character motivation.
2. Claudius says, "My words fly up, my thoughts remain below." What is he admitting?
Answer: B. His prayer is insincere and ineffective.
Standard: RL.9-10.4 – Interpret figurative meaning.
3. What theme is reinforced through Claudius's struggle with guilt?
Answer: B. The moral consequences of sin
Standard: RL.9-10.2 – Theme development.

Short Answer Questions

4. Short Answer: How does Claudius's soliloquy deepen the audience's understanding of his character?
Answer: It shows Claudius is aware of his guilt and desires forgiveness but refuses to give up the benefits of his crime. This complexity reveals him as morally conflicted, not purely evil.
Standard: RL.9-10.3 – Character complexity.
5. Short Answer: What dramatic technique is used when Hamlet hesitates to kill Claudius, and what is its effect?
Answer: Shakespeare uses dramatic irony: the audience knows Claudius is not truly repentant, but Hamlet does not. This increases tension and delays the climax.
Standard: RL.9-10.6 – Analyze point of view and its effect.
