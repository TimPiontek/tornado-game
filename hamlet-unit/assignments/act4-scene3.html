Hamlet Act 4, Scene 3: Claudius Sends Hamlet to England

Scene Summary

Claudius sends Hamlet to England with secret orders for his execution. Hamlet mocks Claudius with riddles about death and decay. Claudius justifies his actions by claiming that Hamlet's madness poses a threat to the kingdom. This scene reveals Claudius's desperation and his willingness to use extreme measures to eliminate Hamlet.

Key Characters in This Scene

  • Claudius - The king, sending Hamlet to his death
  • Hamlet - The prince, mocking Claudius
  • Rosencrantz & Guildenstern - Escorting Hamlet to England

Multiple Choice Questions

1. Claudius says, "Diseases desperate grown / By desperate appliance are relieved." What theme is he invoking to justify his actions?
Answer: B. Extreme corruption requires extreme solutions
Standard: RL.9-10.2 – Analyze theme development.
2. Why does Claudius claim he cannot publicly punish Hamlet?
Answer: B. The queen and the people of Denmark favor Hamlet
Standard: RL.9-10.3 – Analyze political motivations.
3. What is the dramatic irony in Claudius's final lines, "Do it, England"?
Answer: B. Only the audience knows Claudius has ordered Hamlet's death
Standard: RL.9-10.6 – Analyze the impact of dramatic irony.

Short Answer Questions

4. Short Answer: How does Hamlet's interaction with Claudius in this scene reveal his growing boldness?
Answer: Hamlet openly mocks Claudius with riddles about death ("Your worm is your only emperor for diet"), showing that he no longer fears confrontation.
Standard: RL.9-10.3 – Character development.
5. Short Answer: How does this scene escalate the central conflict of the play?
Answer: Claudius's secret order to have Hamlet killed shifts the conflict from secrecy to open assassination plots, escalating the stakes and driving the action toward the climax.
Standard: RL.9-10.3 – Plot development and tension.
